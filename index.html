Predicting Price Paid to Coffee Growers¶

Bryce Dubayah

Globally, coffee is grown in over fifty countries lying between latitudes 25 degrees north and 30 degrees south. 70% of the world's coffee is produced by smallholder farmers, with half living in poverty. These growers rely on the price of coffee (as set by the commodity futures market) being high enough to cover production costs. Rather than the market price, however, the key factor that determines profitability is the amount paid directly to a coffee grower (the 'farmgate' price). While large, commercial farms can easily sell their coffee for market price, small growers or those in rural areas might not be able to, especially if they need to work with a large exporter. In this tutorial, we'll explore data on the coffee futures market and coffee production, examing if there are factors in addition to the market price that affect growers' earnings.

The Data¶

The data used in this tutorial was sourced from the International Coffee Organization (ICO). The ICO works globally with importing and exporting countries to strengthen the coffee sector, with its member countries accounting for 98% of global coffee production and 67% of global coffee consumption. Through these partnerships, the ICO has extensive historical data on coffee supply, trade, and price. We will focus on the ICO datasets on domestic consumption in producing countries, exportable production (the difference of total production and domestic consumption), prices to growers (the average farmgate price paid to a grower), and monthly average coffee futures prices. With the exception of coffee futures prices, these data were reported yearly by country between 1990 and 2017. Units related to green coffee were reported in thousand 60kg bags, and prices were reported in U.S. cents.

Market Prices¶

The first dataset we'll look at contains historical prices for different categories of coffee futures. There are five main categories of coffee traded on the futures market: Colombian Milds, Other Milds, Brazilian Naturals, and Robustas. This dataset contains monthly observations for the prices (in cents) of each of these commodities as well as a composite index developed by the ICO. Since it is an excel spreadsheet, there are also rows used for visual formatting that we can ignore when reading it into a dataframe (via DataFrame's dropna() method.)

Like any commodity, there is high variability in trading prices. Note that, since coffee prices peaked in 2011, there has been a continual downward trend in prices for all categories. This has placed significant pressure on growers who can't turn a profit, and has been termed the coffee price crisis (read more about the crisis at https://sca.coffee/pricecrisis). While all of the coffee categories shown follow the same general trends, it's clear there is some variability between them. Colombian Milds, Other Milds, and Brazilian Naturals are all part of the species C. arabica, while Robustas refer to coffees from the C. canephora species. Robustas have higher caffeine content but worse flavor profiles, and are generally used to make instant coffee for this reason, they trade at prices below the other categories. Arabica coffee is what is sold in coffee shops and grocery stores.

The above graph shows how much coffee is sold for on international exchanges. There are many steps and associated costs between a grower in a coffee producing country and a commodity exchange (transport, export, import, etc. see http://www.ncausa.org/about-coffee/10-steps-from-seed-to-cup for more info) that eat into the money a grower makes. So, how much do coffee growers make relative to the market price?

Prices to growers¶

The ICO also collects data from their member countries on the average price per pound of green coffee paid to growers. This is what is referred to as a "farmgate" price: the actual amount of money that a grower earns. This would vary significantly between growers depending on the quality and type of coffee, and it's hard to imagine that the ICO obtained data from every grower, so we must treat these as rough estimates.

These represent the amount of coffee consumed domestically by producing countries and the amount of coffee available for export, repsectively. Both datasets have the same format, so we can use the same cleaning procedure on both.

We want to transform the data so that every row represents an observation of the quantity produced by a country for a given year. Unlike the prices to growers dataset, each column contains a year range rather than a single year. This is due to how the harvest and growing cycle aligns with the new year. Note that different countries also harvest at different times of the year (and some more than once! - Counter Culture Coffee has a great interactive map to see when harvests occur in each county: https://counterculturecoffee.com/learn/resource-center/coffee-seasonality). We'll also want to remove all of the rows that are group-level aggregates.

In [45]:
def read_production_ico(df):
    series = []
    for index, row in df.iterrows():
        # ignore rows that are aggregates
        if 'group' not in row['Crop year'] and row['Crop year'] != 'Total':
            base = 1990
            years = 2018 - base
            for i in range(years):
                obs = {}
                obs['country'] = row['Crop year']
                obs['quantity'] = row[i + 2]
                obs['year'] = str(base + i)
                series.append(obs)
    return pd.DataFrame(series).dropna(how='any')

At this point, we have the indicator_prices, prices_to_growers, and production datasets. Now, let's use the indicator_prices and production to estimate prices to growers.

Analysis¶

Estimation Strategy¶

Our goal is to predict prices to growers as a function of market prices and production-related variables for a given country. We'll do this using (1) linear regression and (2) random forests. The general relationship for this model will be:

$$PricetoGrowers = ProducerGroup + MarketPrice + DomesticRatio$$

Where ProducerGroup is the primary group of coffee that a country produces, MarketPrice is the market price for the primary kind of coffee that a country produces, and DomesticRatio is the ratio of domestic production to total production of a country. For linear regression, we will have to transform each of the producer groups into a separate binary variable, and market price into an interaction term between each binary producer group variable and its associated market price. The fully specified linear regression equation will have the form:

$$PricetoGrowers = _1*IsColombianMild + _2*IsOtherMild + _3*IsBrazilianNatural + _4*IsRobusta + _5*IsColombianMild*ColombianMild + _6*IsOtherMild*OtherMild B_7*IsBrazilianNatural*BrazilianNatural + _8*IsRobusta*Robusta + _9*DomesticRatio$$

Note that we don't include a constant term, as it would create a collinear relationship between the binary variables for producer group. Let's transform the data so we can run the regression.

Random Forests¶

Random forests is a machine learning method based on decision trees. Rather than creating a single tree, it creates many trees (in our case, 1000) and averages their predictions. For a more comprehensive overview, see the Wikipedia entry: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_forest.

Final Thoughts¶

Clearly, there are many complex factors affecting the farmgate price paid to coffee growers, many of which are likely not contained in the data collected by the ICO. While it's intuitive that the market price of coffee should affect the amount that growers are paid, the importance of the ratio of domestic consumption to production is not as clear. It's possible that countries that consume more of the coffee they produce share commonalities in terms of wage structure and worker compensation. Or, growers may be able to sell directly to in-country coffee roasters rather than working with large, multinational coffee exporters that need to guarantee a profit.
